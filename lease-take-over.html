Lease Take Over: Your Way Out of a Car Lease

The idea of a car lease is to have the opportunity to use a car and, in essence, pay for the value of the car according to your time of usage. For instance, when you get a two-year car lease on a $20,000 vehicle, you will only pay for the difference of the car’s principal amount and the car’s depreciated value. This is a more convenient option for people who want to use a car without having to pay for the hefty monthly bills. They don’t get to own the car—hence, their deal has no equity or resale value (primarily because the car wasn’t purchased in the first place)—but they don’t have to deal with high monthly charges and owning a property that, quite frankly, cannot be considered as an investment.

However, no matter how cheaper it is to get a car lease, there are instances when people have to get out of a car lease. Legally, getting out of a car lease is possible—one will just have to pay hefty penalties, making the lease cancellation moot and pointless. Why cancel a lease when you can pay for the expensive penalties anyway? Fortunately, there is another way out of a car lease—the lease take over.

The lease take over—also known as swap a lease—is exactly what the term describes. It allows you to get out of a lease by passing the payment obligations of the lease to another person—naturally, someone who wants to get a car through leasing. Needless to say, it is a flexible and somewhat practical way of ending a car lease earlier than what is indicated in the contract. Simply put, instead of finishing the lease, the other person gets of what remains of the lease period (meaning, he can use the car for that long). Of course, he gets the financial obligations as well. It is a transfer of the lease ownership. If the new lease owner wants to extend the lease after the lease take over or wants to own the car by the end of the lease, it is already his call.

There are, of course, some repercussions before engaging into this kind of transaction. First, it should be noted that not all leasing companies allow a lease take over within any point of the contract. Meanwhile, there are others that do not allow the lease takeover during the last few days of the contract. Either way, you should try to consider this before settling with a particular car leasing company. After all, you would not know whether certain circumstances will prevent you from finishing your car lease. You wouldn’t want to be stuck with the lease even when you cannot.

Also, a lease take over means the transfer of lease ownership. However, some leasing companies make the original owner of the car lease liable when the new owner fails to finish the car lease or when the new owner fails to make the payments. This is the reason why many consider the lease take over as something that should be done between relatives or close relations only. You wouldn’t want to risk paying for the lease when you aren’t even using the car!

On the other side of the fence, is a lease take over practical? It definitely is—in fact, it is more practical for them than for the ones who original own the car lease. And the reason is simple: when one takes over a lease, he only takes the responsibility of paying for the remaining monthly payments. However, he wouldn’t have to pay for the other fees that the original lease owner made. Granted, a car lease does not require a deposit, but it still involves the payment of various fees—from security deposits to financial charge. You wouldn’t be able to get that back so, in a way, this can be considered as your penalty for not finishing the loan contract.

Just the same, a lease take over remains to be a practical choice when you have no choice but to give up lease ownership. Lease contracts can be very difficult to deal with, especially when you fail to finish your obligations. As a way out of legal troubles, the lease take over is a very wise choice.
